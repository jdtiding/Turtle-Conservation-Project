Overview Overview

Location Location

Seeking to mitigate the looting of turtle nests and increasing the well-being of the local turtle population, the Vida VerdiAzul Association has launched the Pacific Leatherback Turtle Conservation Project to ensure optimal conditions for turtle nesting through active conservation with community support.

We are located on Junquillal Beach on Pacific Coast of Costa Rica in the northern Guanacaste Province. Junquillal is a small, pleasant village with a strong community of 220 inhabitants. Its main attractions are the beaches that extend for 5 km and the mangrove forest which is home to unique flora and fauna.

Playa Junquillal is one of the most remote and pristine beaches of Guanacaste. It also has environmental value as a nesting site for Leatherback Turtle. However, Junquillal is not part of the National System of Protected Areas of Costa Rica. This means that the three species of turtles: Olive Ridley, Green and Leatherback, that come ashore to lay their eggs are very vulnerable to poachers.

Illegal egg harvesting was once rampant on Junquillal beach. Since 2010 the Vida VerdiAzul Association has been taking action to protect these eggs.

Info Olive Ridley Turtle

The Olive Ridley Turtle is the most common and the least endangered species, smaller than 70 cm, with six to eight pleural plates on each side, olive-green and broad-headed. Although it can nest throughout the year, they arrive in Junquillal in great numbers between August and December. They lay about 100 eggs in a nest and incubation period lasts between 46 and 50 days.

Info Green Turtle

Although in the last century the Green Turtle (aka Black Turtle) was abundant in Junquillal, the destruction of their habitats and the collection of their eggs place it as the second most endangered species. It usually reaches the length of one meter, has four pleural plates per side, oval and dark shell but with color variations between individuals and proportionately small head and round profiles. They nest in Junquillal during most of the year, although more frequently between September and April. They lay between 65 and 80 eggs in a nest and have an incubation period of about 50 - 55 days.

Info Leatherback Turtle

The Leatherback is the largest of the turtles and by far the most endangered, with its Pacific population in critical danger of extinction. It is characterized by the presence of a soft and smooth shell covered by seven ridges that run longitudinally. Its front fins are proportionally larger than in other species of sea turtles. They nest in Junquillal between the end of October and March. They lay an average of 65 eggs in a nest and have an incubation period of 55-60 days.

For who

Volunteer profile Volunteer profile

If you're looking for meaningful travel opportunities that put the focus on marine conservation or you would simply like to explore the world in a responsible and positive way.

If you like to travel, meet new people and learn about ecology and other cultures, you might like to donate part of your time and contribute to a conservation program.

If you are a pensioner and would love to contribute to a project where you have the opportunity to be in contact with the environment and can also join forces to help conserve it.

Volunteering with sea turtles is a major step towards their conservation and doubles as an enriching experience for yourself. Not only will you gain knowledge to share with your community and make a positive difference, but you’ll also be part of an adventure that you'll be able to share at home.

We are open to all volunteers that have an interest in the protection of sea turtles and are ready to come to Junqillal with an open mind and positive attitude.

Requirements Requirements

To join our program you need to:

  • be over 18 years old;
  • be dynamic, proactive and eager to work and learn;
  • love nature;
  • enjoy working outdoors, even in the sun or at night;
  • have good physical condition;
  • collaborate at least 15 days;
  • present to work according to the agreed day with the program;
  • comply with a schedule of 5 to 8 hours a day, depending on the tasks assigned to you for 5 days a week;
  • have medical insurance (mandatory);
  • no prior knowledge is necessary, just the desire to learn;

Activities Activities

General description General description

When volunteering in our Sea Turtle Conservation Project you will work alongside a biologist to assist with sea turtle studies and support sustainable egg protection programs.

Protecting these spectacular species will be at the heart of all your volunteer work, as you give-back with other international volunteers from around the world.

Sea turtles are a fundamental link in marine ecosystems and as a marine life conservation volunteer, you’ll be working to protect and rehabilitate endangered sea turtle species.

Volunteers, in addition to demonstrating their environmental awareness will have the opportunity to support social projects that are carried out in the local community to improve the quality of life of their inhabitants.

Daily activities Daily activities

Your role on the project will vary according to the time of the year, but you’ll work a variety of day and night shifts, which may include:

  • night beach patrols;
  • locating nesting females;
  • collecting eggs;
  • building hatcheries to protect eggs from predators;
  • counting and tagging sea turtles;
  • monitoring protected nests;
  • ensuring that the born turtles arrive alive at sea;
  • replanting mangroves;
  • beach cleaning;

About Us

About Us

Who are We Who we are

Our association is an independent non-government non-profit group born in 2010. Our goal is to ensure the conservation of threatened ecosystems and contribute to the well-being of the communities that depend on them. Our headquarters are located in Junquillal Beach in Santa Cruz, Costa Rica and since 2001 we have been working for the conservation of sea turtles and the Nandamojo River basin.

History History

The first scientific research with sea turtles in Junquillal was carried out between 2001 and 2004 as part of the thesis work of Gabriel Francia for the Wildlife Program of the National University of Heredia, Costa Rica. Carlos Drews, back then coordinator of the WWF marine species program for Latin America, proposed to Gabriel to start a conservation project for leatherback turtles, a species with frequent nesting in Junquillal.

Since 2000 the population of leatherbacks in the Eastern Pacific has been showing an alarming decline, a situation that currently places these species on the brink of extinction. In Junquillal until 2004, the most obvious threat to leatherbacks and other turtle species was the indiscriminate looting of their nests for the consumption and sale of their eggs. Due to this reason, a program with a strong social base was started, facilitating the approach of the community to the conservation of sea turtles. Thus, in December 2004, the Pacific Leatherback Conservation Project (LCP), administered by WWF was born. In mid - 2005, the environmental education program that encompassed three communities was launched and in November a group of local youths were set up to guard the beaches of Junquillal to protect the nests and would be known as the Baula Boys. In 2007, the LCP began the Climate Change Research Program and in 2009 the first climate change mitigation measures were implemented on the coast of Junquillal.

Finally, in June 2010, WWF transferred the LCP project to a local Junquillal group, thus creating our VerdiAzul Life Conservation Association.
